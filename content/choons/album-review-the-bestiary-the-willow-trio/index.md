For the site's first review, I thought I'd write a little about the album I've been listening to the most this week: *[The Bestiary](https://thewillowtrio.bandcamp.com/album/the-bestiary-2)* by The Willow Trio.

This is the trio's second album and is a beautiful arrangement of harps---creating a dreamy whirlpool of sounds to float around in. Due to the instrumental nature of the album, it's hard for me to suggest a well-defined interpretation of what the music is saying to me. My memory from [Disney's Fantasia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fantasia_(1940_film)) is that instrumental pieces often fit into one of three categories: those which tell a definite story; those without plot that still create a series of definite pictures; and those which simply exist for their own sake. For me, I'd say the album fits nicely into that second category.

The Willow Trio themselves suggest that the album is about nature, art, and the little things in life that make you want to write songs.

And cats.

Actually, the liner notes do help paint a series of pictures for us. They range from songs about babies, stereo sound, extinct toads, and ferry transport on the west coast of Scotland. While I can't speak to any of that, I can say that I found the album relaxing and engaging, with stellar performances and composition. None of my interpretations have anything to do with the intent of the composers, and simply tell of what was in my head as I listened.

The album is bookended by the *Oystercatchers* theme. It does indeed feel like sitting near a slightly unforgiving body of water. Alert, but content to relax and watch what comes. The reprise at the end of the album feels a little more insistent as if the tide is coming in and it's time to pack up the picnic blanket.

*Ink Spills* gives me the feeling I get when I know I have things to be getting on with, but now I've sat down in my comfy chair and I'm not sure getting up will really be worth it. It feels like the debate I have in my mind about whether feeling good now will outweigh feeling bad later. The final notes seem to suggest a decision has been made (in my case, it was probably to get up).

The third track, *Miss Mollie Moo*, made me think about a kingfisher darting in and out of the river, with periods of quiet surveillance to look for the next catch. For some reason, this is the only track in which my imaginings appeared as animation rather than real life. Take from that what you will.

I think *Jasper* is the first piece where we hear sounds made by something other than a harp. A rhythmic shaker and some other light percussion that stops and starts throughout suggest some structured physical action being carried out beyond the more carefree meanderings of the mind. I imagine myself walking down the street with all the weird thoughts in my mind, but trying to keep my walk smooth and unremarkable so that I don't draw attention to myself. And yet, every now and then, I let a bit of a silly walk into my steps, like a pressure release to allow me to return to normal.

An odd vision entered my head when listening to *The Gentle Giant*. I imagined a Studio Ghibli styled cat wandering around a rural Japanese community. The framing, movement, and actions all seemed to match that style but, oddly, it was all live action. It's a sweet little piece at any rate. It definitely made me think of someone exploring their domain with safe reassurance that all is well (and anything that seems like it isn't is probably just a misunderstanding).

*A Similar Gait* was the only track on the album that made me feel slightly unnerved. It's not creepy, or anything like that. It's more like it's describing someone that isn't entirely sure that they've not shown up to their job interview on the wrong day.

The seventh piece on the record is inspired by the work of one of my favourite Glasgow artists, Margaret Macdonald-Mackintosh. *The Mysterious Garden* is not a painting that I have ever seen in person (though I'm told it currently resides in one of Scotland's National Galleries of Modern Art in Edinburgh). As such, I can't say whether or not this piece captures the feeling of witnessing the artwork in person, but I can say that it's a dreamlike piece. It feels a little like being lost in misty streets at night. It feels like the streets all look the same, yet the light only comes from one direction in the sky.

As we near the end of the album, it feels like perhaps we're trying to hold onto things just a little longer. Something about this piece makes me think of someone looking for a way to carry on and complete the task laid forth. I'm not well versed enough in the behaviour of a *Golden Toad* (extinct or otherwise) to know how well that feeling lines up, but in my mind it felt like we were actually succeeding and might still pull off whatever it is we're trying to achieve. It makes me think of determination and drive and resolve. If I wanted to psyche myself up to finish writing a blog article, I can imagine this being the music of my mind.

The album ends with a return to the *Oystercatchers* theme (this time alternately titled, *I'm Not Getting On A Boat!*). I feel like there's definitely a story behind that one, but alas I'm not intelligent enough to decipher it.

*The Bestiary* is a delightful and relaxing album that I can see myself reaching for on any days I want to sit inside and stare out at the rain. It's a warm little comfort blanket for the mind.

You can buy [The Bestiary](https://thewillowtrio.bandcamp.com/album/the-bestiary-2) now.
