Many years ago, I recorded some music at home with my band. We didn't know what we were doing, we had no money, and there was no one to guide us. I was pretty adept with computers and brimming with youthful confidence, so I tried to fake a recording studio at home using just my desktop and some decent microphones. It would be another five or ten years before that became a common approach. Today we'd call that 'in the box' music production.

Despite our lack of resources or knowledge, our trial-and-error approach yielded decent enough results to release some music into the world.

I wish I'd had some of today's tools available to me back then because music production has moved on a lot! But what I really wish is that there had been a good introduction to *in the box* music production that wouldn't have cost an arm and a leg to learn and use. With this site, I'm hoping to create that resource for people just getting started today.

However, I'm not an expert. I'm going to do my best to make this resource as accurate as possible, but I'm still figuring it out myself. The upside is that I'll see things from a newcomer's perspective and be better suited to tell you what you need to know. I won't make any assumptions about your knowledge, and I'll try to avoid getting bogged down in unnecessary theory and technicalities (though some of that can't be avoided).

I want this site to be an answer to the question that I most wanted to know when I was starting out:

> ***How do I quickly make and release well-produced music?***

I'm not expecting this site to become a masterclass in music production, but I want it to cover everything you need to answer that question. Think of it as a primer to get you going as quickly as possible. I'll probably never cover everything about *in the box* music production, and even the things I do share are not going to be the last word on this stuff. This isn't going to be the only way (it may not even be the 'correct' way), but it will always be a viable way.

If you want to learn everything about music production and are willing to spend the time doing so, then I commend you and wish you well. These posts might still help you get started, but my focus is on getting people to a 'professional' sound as quickly and cheaply as possible. I'll explain why I've done things the way I've done them, but I won't explain all the other possibilities and how they work. I'm trying to save you the years of research that got me to my goal of cheap, professional-sounding releases.

And just to be clear: this is going to be for *digital* audio and music production. *Analogue* production has a totally different approach. Digital is definitely the way to go for home musicians these days because the quality you can get for the money you put in is truly phenomenal. It simply wouldn't be possible with analogue systems at these price points. That's not to say that you can't make your digital recordings *sound* like analogue... but we'll get to that later.

There is one last thing I want to talk about here, because it's key to my approach:

As far as possible, I'll be sticking to open source software and plugins. The reason for this is that the software my band used back in the day was proprietary and I can no longer open those files because the software company no longer supports them. When using proprietary software, you are always opening yourself up to the possibility of your access to files being restricted, or files no longer being supported: all at the whim of a third party that is focused more on economics than on your files. This should be far less likely using open source software.

Open source software is also more likely to work across operating systems, can usually be used without spending any money, can be archived, and generally doesn't spy on me while I'm using it.

Although industry-standard software is largely proprietary (and I'll use some of it), I'll favor open-source alternatives whenever they offer similar quality.

And just to head this one off at the pass: using open source software to make music does not make your music open source. It has no effect whatsoever on the ownership or license possibilities of what you create. It astounds me how many people avoid using open source software over this, spending much more money on tools that are no better than what they could have had for free.

I guess that's the plan, then. Hopefully we'll all be making music together before too long. Grab your computer, pull out your best song, and let's get started.
